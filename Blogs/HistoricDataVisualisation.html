Analysis: History Of Data Visualisation

Posted Tuesday, 11 August

In this blog post, I will be analysing Florence Nightingales Diagram of the causes of mortality in the army in the East. Nightingale was a well-known statistician, social reformer, and founder of modern nursing often referred to as “the lady with the lamp” (ASA 2020). In her time, Nightingale was a manager and trainer of nurses during the Crimean Wars, who pioneered data visualisation with the use of infographics.

In the 1850s, during the Crimean War, the soldier’s mortality rate was very high and ever-increasing. Whilst working as a manager and visiting various hospitals, Nightingale found that majority of the deaths were caused by poor hospital practices (Tableau 2020). It was during this period that she was inspired to research and thus, create the chart that would represent and compare the soldier’s deaths caused by war and those by poor hospital hygiene.

Nightingale’s visualisation was in a book she called “Coxcombs” (Visually 2020), the name which later was adopted as the name of the actual chart. The coxcomb chart is a diagram that is similar to a pie chart however providing an opportunity for more data detail. The main difference is that with a pie chart the size of the slices represents the data, whereas, the coxcomb uses slices that extend in length to represent data. On the coxcomb, the data has various layers that extend with the length of the slice. Nightingales chart had a total of 12 slices, that represented the months of the year. Each slice is divided into portions that represent various categories of data. For example, in Nightingales' chart, the lightly shaded areas represented total deaths, and the darker shaded areas represented deaths caused by battles. (Tableau 2020).

Besides being the chart that helped Nightingale pioneer data visualisation, as well as, making her a role model to many in the field of statistics. The coxcomb chart helped her emphasize the need for increased hospital sanitation and overall hygiene(ASA 2020). Through this, she went on to save many soldier’s lives.

According to the World Health Organisation, “the occurrence of adverse events due to unsafe care is likely one of the 10 leading causes of death and disability in the world.” (WHO 2019). Furthermore, it has been found that “134 million adverse events occur in hospitals in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), due to unsafe care, resulting in 2.6 million deaths.” (WHO 2019). These statistics are based on 2019 findings. During the current pandemic, alongside unsafe care in rural areas, the lack of equipment and capacity has resulted in many deaths. In the time of the Crimean battles, Nightingale collected data that would reflect the importance of sanitation in hospitals, moreover, that visualised the relationship between cleanliness and death tolls(Tableau 2020). As a result, not only was the data visualisation in itself important but so was the research and data that formed it.

The coxcomb chart is a suitable choice for presentation purposes, especially, to communicate to viewers who don’t have much technical knowledge regarding the data. On the other hand, coxcomb charts are not ideal for representing subtle changes and differences, as these are difficult to detect and represent. They are, however, ideal for demonstrating patterns present within the dataset.

Reflecting on Jenny Kidd’s theories of representation, I can identify, as Kidd advances, that signs and text in themselves don’t have meaning rather obtain their meaning by virtue of its difference from other signs and text. That is to say, although the rising soldier’s mortality was a concern, the extent of the issue was better established a defined by how the death toll of war-related incidents and healthcare-related incidents were compared to each other.

Although having lost a sense of its aura through mass reproduction, which as explained by Benjamin Walter and asserted by Kidd, was as a result of reproduced work “lacking in one element; its presence in time and space its unique existence at the place where it happens to be” (Benjamin, 1955:29). The coxcomb chart still reflects the politics of representation, as it was created to persuade. Kidd stated that “to say something is political is to note that it can never be neutral or a-historical, even when it might appear to be ambivalent.” (Kidd, 2015:37).

Regardless of the intention, any form of representation can be political as its open for interpretation. In the case of Nightingale, her chart challenged the English healthcare system, questioning the policies and procedures that had been put in place in hospitals. In her article, Sujata Gupta emphasizes that Nightingale took advantage of the power of visualising science to “convince the general public, medical staff, and lawmakers that sanitation saves lives.” (Gupta 2020)
